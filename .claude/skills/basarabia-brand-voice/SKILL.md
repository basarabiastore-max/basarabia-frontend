# Basarabia Brand Voice Skill

This skill captures the brand identity of Basarabia Concept Store — a premium Eastern European grocery concept store in Spalding, UK, founded by Andrei (a former Romanian lawyer). It also applies to Sybil Technologies products that wear the Basarabia brand as a demo/reference implementation.

When this skill is active, every piece of copy, every design choice, every micro-decision should pass the test: **"Would this feel at home alongside Tanti Olguța's video and the unified manifesto?"** If no, rewrite.

---

## Core brand identity

**What Basarabia is:**
- A Concept Store, not a supermarket. Curated, not comprehensive. Every product is chosen deliberately.
- Three operations under one roof: butcher (in-house meat cutting), baker, pastry chef (in-house bread and pastries made fresh every morning).
- A wide Eastern European range: Romanian, Moldovan, Bulgarian, Polish, Lithuanian, Latvian, Ukrainian products plus UK essentials.
- Positioned as a £15–50k-quality portfolio piece — designed to later be licensed or cloned for other diaspora shops via Sybil Technologies.
- Physical shop in Spalding (Ground Floor, 11 Market Place, PE11 1SL) with 20-mile delivery radius covering Holbeach, Bourne, Boston, Peterborough.

**Target audience:**
- Primary: Romanian and Moldovan diaspora in the UK — especially those who lived through communism or grew up with grandmothers who cooked traditional food.
- Secondary: broader Eastern European diaspora (Polish, Ukrainian, Bulgarian, Lithuanian, Latvian).
- Tertiary: curious English/British customers drawn to authentic food stores.
- NOT the target: budget shoppers, young single professionals looking for convenience. They may buy, but they're not whom we write for.

**Emotional core:**
Memory. Home. The specific ache diaspora feel when they taste or smell something from childhood. The reverence for a grandmother's cooking. The pride of eating food your culture made, not food adapted for the English palate. Never sentimental to the point of being maudlin — confident and warm.

## The character: Tanti Olguța

Tanti Olguța (formerly "Mamaie") is the face of Basarabia. AI-generated character, traditional Romanian dress, white embroidered blouse with red floral motifs, headscarf, warm smile. She emerges from darkness holding a carved wooden BASARABIA sign in the hero video.

- Address: "Tanti Olguța" in copy, never "Mamaie" (the word "mamaie" is kept exclusively for the user-facing greeting *"Bine ai venit, Mamaie!"* which is a Moldovan term of endearment a grandmother uses for her grandchild, not the character's name).
- In recipe content and videos, she speaks warmly and with the unhurried cadence of an elderly woman who knows what she's doing — not rushed, not performative, not "youthful influencer" energy.
- Her voice is the voice of *home*. If a line sounds like it could come from any generic grandma character, rewrite it to be more specifically Romanian/Moldovan.

## Romanian copywriting rules

**Voice:**
- Confident, not humble. Never apologise for being small. Never suggest effort ("we work hard"). State what IS.
- Warm without being saccharine. Reverent without being pious.
- Use diminutives sparingly — "bucățică," "borcănelul" — they add warmth but overuse is cloying.
- Use the informal "tu" (address to a single reader as an equal, never "dumneavoastră" in marketing copy — that's for business emails and customer service).

**Vocabulary:**
- "Gustul de acasă" — the taste of home. Central phrase. Reuse deliberately.
- "Cămara bunicii" — grandmother's pantry. Core emotional image.
- "Drumul spre casă" — the road home.
- "Rețete autentice" — authentic recipes. Never "tradiționale" alone, always paired with specific context (traditional to where? by whom?).
- "Fără compromisuri" — no compromises. Strong, defensible phrase.
- Avoid: "calitate superioară," "cel mai bun," "premium" — generic marketing language. Show, don't claim.

**Structure:**
- Short lines that breathe.
- Full sentences with periods, not commas. Rhythm matters.
- Repetition as a device: *"E memoria unui gust. E drumul spre casă. E..."* — the anaphora pattern works well in Romanian and feels liturgical in a good way.
- Poetic openings, operational middles, poetic closings — the pattern established in the unified manifesto.

**Things to NEVER write in Romanian copy:**
- Founder-failure narratives (culturally reads as weakness in Romanian/Moldovan audiences — they want strength and trajectory, not vulnerability).
- Direct comparisons to competitors by name.
- Discount/urgency language ("doar astăzi," "reducere mare"). Basarabia isn't a deal shop.
- Jokes. The brand is warm and serious. Never ironic or clever at its own expense.
- Foreign words where a Romanian word exists (say "magazin," not "shop"; "livrare," not "delivery"). EXCEPTION: "Concept Store" is kept in English because it's the positioning phrase and functions as proper-noun branding.

## English copywriting rules

English copy is always the secondary voice — a respectful translation for customers who don't read Romanian, not the primary message. Visually smaller, muted, italicised where appropriate.

**Voice:**
- Translate the *meaning*, not the words. A literal translation from Romanian often reads stilted in English.
- Keep it understated. English text should feel like you overhearing a private conversation in Romanian — translated for you as a courtesy, not targeted at you as a sales pitch.
- Never try to be more emotional in English than in Romanian. Same temperature.

**Vocabulary parallels:**
- "Gustul de acasă" → "the taste of home" (not "authentic flavour" or "traditional cuisine")
- "Cămara bunicii tale" → "your grandmother's pantry" (NOT "your grandma's cupboard" — pantry carries more weight)
- "Concept Store" stays in English
- "Bine ai venit, Mamaie!" does NOT translate well. In English contexts, use "Welcome home" or leave the Romanian untranslated.

## Visual design rules

**Color palette (exact values):**
- Background black: `#000000` (pure black for hero, manifesto, most sections)
- Section separator black: `#0a0a0a` (for "De Ce Noi?" and footer — subtly lighter)
- Brand gold: `#D4A017` (primary accent, headings, borders, CTAs)
- Cream/ivory: `#F5E6C8` (body highlights, prominent headings)
- Muted gold body text: `#A8957A` (body paragraphs, navigation links)
- Muted secondary: `#7a6a54` (footnotes, small print, tertiary text)
- Burgundy accent: `#8B1A1A` (used only in the top/bottom gold-burgundy gradient strips)

**Typography hierarchy:**
- Cinzel Decorative: prominent headings, brand name, section openers. The display font.
- Georgia (or "Palatino Linotype" / "Times New Roman" fallback): body text, paragraphs. Warm serif, readable.
- Italic Georgia: final emotional lines, footnotes, subheads.
- Arial / sans-serif: ONLY for tiny utility text (eyebrows, labels), letter-spaced at 0.3-0.4em, uppercase.
- NEVER: rounded modern sans-serifs (Inter, Poppins, Circular, Roboto). They betray the heritage aesthetic.

**Typography sizing (clamp pattern):**
- Display headings: `clamp(1.8rem, 3.8vw, 3.4rem)`
- Section headings: `clamp(1.25rem, 3vw, 1.9rem)`
- Body prominent: `clamp(0.95rem, 1.9vw, 1.15rem)`
- Body standard: `clamp(0.9rem, 1.6vw, 1.05rem)`
- Footnotes: `clamp(0.78rem, 1.4vw, 0.88rem)`
- Eyebrow labels: `0.6rem`–`0.88rem`, letter-spacing `0.3em`–`0.42em`, uppercase

**Spacing and rhythm:**
- Section vertical padding (hero-adjacent narrative sections): `clamp(120px, 14vw, 200px)`
- Section vertical padding (information sections like De Ce Noi?, Footer): `60-80px`
- Max content width for text-heavy sections: `700px`
- Max content width for grid sections: `1100-1200px`
- Generous whitespace is the point. When in doubt, add padding.

**Motion principles:**
- Framer Motion everywhere, with `useInView({ once: true })` for scroll-triggered reveals.
- Easing: prefer `[0.22, 1, 0.36, 1]` (smooth ease-out-expo) for anything that feels cinematic.
- Duration: 0.7-1.2s for most reveals. Slower than typical web motion. Adds reverence.
- Stagger: 0.3-0.8s between sibling elements for sequential reveals.
- NEVER: bouncy springs, elastic effects, aggressive scale-ups. Wrong vibe.

**Decorative elements:**
- `✦` (gold) — primary ornament, used as section dividers and accent flourishes
- `✿` (gold) — secondary ornament, used sparingly (e.g. "Tradițional" card in De Ce Noi?)
- Short horizontal gold gradient lines flanking ornaments: `linear-gradient(to right, transparent, #D4A017)` at 1px height, 36px width.

## Compositional patterns

**Bilingual sections follow this exact pattern:**
1. Romanian block (full, styled prominent)
2. Centered `✦` divider with ~3rem vertical space
3. English block (smaller, more muted, italic where the Romanian is not)

**Every new narrative section should have:**
- One prominent opening line (Cinzel Decorative, cream, large)
- One or two supporting paragraphs (Georgia, muted gold)
- An italic closing line (gold, smaller) when the section needs emotional punctuation
- Cinematic reveal animation tied to scroll via `useInView`

**When in doubt about a new section's structure:**
Match the unified manifesto's structure: opening → body → poetic closing. It's the canonical pattern.

## What NOT to build

- Carousels with auto-advance — cheap aesthetic, wrong brand
- Modal popups for newsletter signup or promos — interrupts the reverent experience
- Animated parallax backgrounds that move faster than 0.3x scroll — motion should serve calm, not stimulate
- Toast notifications with emoji — brand voice is serious, no thumbs-up 👍
- Comic Sans. Obviously. But also: any font that wasn't approved above.
- Bright accent colors (green, blue, purple). The palette is black, gold, cream, burgundy. That's it.
- Pricing callouts ("£X.99!!") with exclamation marks. Prices are stated plainly.
- Social proof counters ("10,000 customers!") — not the brand.

## When writing copy, always:

1. Start by asking: *"What is this section emotionally asking the reader to feel?"* Then write to that emotion, not to the information.
2. Write the Romanian first. Always. Then translate.
3. Read it aloud in your head in a Romanian grandmother's voice. If it sounds wrong in her voice, rewrite.
4. Cut every adjective that isn't earning its place.
5. Preserve rhythm — short lines break deliberately, long lines flow.
6. Check the vocabulary rules above before shipping. "Gustul de acasă" is approved. "Authentic premium flavours" is banned.

---

## When making visual decisions, always:

1. Check the color palette. No hex codes outside the approved set without explicit brand approval.
2. Check the typography. Cinzel for display, Georgia for body. Nothing else except utility Arial for micro-labels.
3. Check spacing. More is almost always better than less.
4. Check motion. Slow, smooth, reverent. Never snappy or bouncy.
5. Compare to the existing hero, manifesto, and delivery zones sections. If your new section feels visually alien next to them, it's wrong.

## Reference for Sybil licensing

When this brand voice skill is used for a non-Basarabia client (e.g. a Polish shop licensing the Sybil storefront), the structural rules stay identical but the cultural specifics adapt:
- Character name and cultural context change (Babcia instead of Tanti Olguța, etc.)
- Palette and typography may be adjusted per client brief
- The core principle — reverent, culturally-specific, diaspora-aware — stays
- Never copy-paste Romanian phrases into other cultural contexts. "Cămara bunicii" is Basarabia's. A Polish shop gets its own equivalent, in Polish, chosen by someone who knows Polish diaspora culture intimately.
